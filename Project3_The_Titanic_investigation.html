Background: RMS Titanic

Titanic was the largest ship afloat at the time of her sailing. On April 10, 1912, she made her maiden voyage. Five days later, on April 15, 1912, she sank after colliding with an iceberg, killing 1502 out of 2224 passengers and crew on board. These figures translate to a 32% survival rate.

One of the biggest reason the shipwreck caused such huge loss of life, was the shortage of lifeboats for the passengers and crew. Those who were lucky enough to survive were largely either women or children maybe because of "women and children first" policy, or they might have belonged to the upper-class.

I am going to be analyizing The Titanic dataset that contains demographics and passenger information from about 40% (891 of the 2224) passengers and crew on board the Titanic.

In summary, out of the 891 passengers on board, 35% (314) were females and 65% (577) were males. There is a clear indication that females had a far better survival rate than males. About 74%, 233 out of 314 females survived, while only about 19%, 109 out of 577 males could survive.

Passengers with titles including Miss, Master, and Mrs further suggest, that females and children had better chances of survival than older males. Hopefully, a comparison by age will further shed some light on this assumption.

As I mentioned, the proportion of females survivors compared to males survivors was far greater. One reason for this difference could have been the "women and children first" policy that might have allowed more women to be saved.
To test whether this difference had any statistical significance I'll conduct a one tailed z test, at an alpha level of 0.01. M will be proportion of male survivals, F will be proportion of female survivals.

Null hypothesis for this test is: proportion of female to male survivors is equal. $$H_o: F_{proportion} = M_{proportion}$$

Alternate hypothesis for this test is: proportion of female survivors is greater than male survivors. $$H_a: F_{proportion} > M_{proportion}$$

In [22]:
# importing math library
import math
# setting up sample size and means
f_sample = 314
F_proportion = round(0.7420382165605095, 4)

m_sample = 577
M_proportion = round(0.18890814558058924, 4)

# calculating pooled variance
pooled_variance = (F_proportion * f_sample + M_proportion * m_sample) / (f_sample + m_sample)

# calculating standard error
std_err = math.sqrt(pooled_variance * (1 - pooled_variance) * ((1/f_sample) + 1/m_sample))

# calculating Z score
z_score = round((F_proportion - M_proportion)/std_err, 2)
print("Z score for the test is:",z_score)

Z score for the test is: 16.22

Since 16.22 > 2.33, I reject the null that proportions are equal. There is indeed, statistical evidence that if a passenger were to be a female, her chances of survival were greater.

Limitations

Considering that the size of the dataset was fairly small and almost all of the data was analyized using vectorized operations, the computational cost for analyzing this dataset was fairly low. Therefore, I did not find computational cost limiting my analysis in a meaningful way. While the dataset was small, the data within it was fairly rich. The sample size and number of variables were sufficient enough to let me perform a detailed analysis. Therefore, the data itself was not limiting.

However, one aspect of the data that I did find limiting, was the lack of information regarding all the missing values. I did not know why those values were missing. Was the missingness completely at random or were there other reasons? Because I was not sure why those values were missing I was not able to prepare a better model to impute missing values in the data. If I had known the cause of missingness, I would have made a better model for substituting missing values that would have led to a more robust analysis.

Conclusion

What factors made it more likely for a person to survive on the Titanic? That was the question that kickstarted this investigation. I think, I have enough evidence to answer this question now. According to this analysis, there were three probable factors that could have affected survival.

1) Gender: could have been the most important factor in survival of passengers on board, in my opinion. The women and children first policy might have allowed for 74% survival rate for women compared to 19 % survival rate for men.

2) Pclass: revealed that there was a strong relationship between a passengers' survival and the class she was travelling in. 63% first class travellers survived compared to only 24% that travelled in the third class. This is, pretty much, a clear indication that Pclass might have been another factor in survival.

3) Fare: also revealed a strong relationship with survival. The higher a customer paid the better chances of survival she had.
